## Register

brand

## Users

Three audiences, confirmed as equally important:

1. **Prospective attendees** — young builders, operators, and founders in NYC deciding whether to come to a mixer, dinner, or gathering. They land here from a friend's link or a Luma page and need to feel, within seconds, that these are their people.
2. **Prospective residents** — people considering applying to live in the house for a summer cohort. They're evaluating whether the community is real, warm, and worth uprooting a summer for.
3. **Sponsors & partners** — companies and investors deciding whether to back or host with the community. They need proof of caliber: who shows up, what happens at events, which brands are already involved.

All three arrive in an evaluative mood; the site is the community's proof of life.

## Product Purpose

Chelsea Commons is a community of young, ambitious builders, operators, and founders in New York. It started as twelve people living together in a house at 300 W 20th Street in Chelsea and is growing into a network that hosts dinners, mixers, and events across the city, with a new resident cohort each summer.

The site exists to make the community legible and joinable. Success means: people RSVP to events, strong candidates reach out about future cohorts, and sponsors/partners come away convinced this is a real, high-caliber community — all in roughly equal measure. Every page should give each audience an obvious next step without splitting the site into separate funnels.

## Brand Personality

*(Inferred from the codebase and site copy — user was away for this interview round; confirm or revise.)*

**Warm, ambitious, unpretentious.** The voice of a group chat that became a house: personal and direct ("If you want to be around people who make things happen, you'll fit in"), never corporate. The visual register is warm editorial — serif italic wordmark, paper-cream surfaces, halftone photo treatments, candid event photography — a neighborhood institution being built in real time by people in their early twenties. Emotional goals: belonging, credibility, and a little FOMO.

## Anti-references

*(Inferred; confirm or revise.)*

- **SaaS startup landing pages** — hero metrics, feature grids, gradient CTAs, "Join 10,000+ builders" social-proof mechanics. This is a community, not a product with a conversion funnel.
- **Hacker-house / accelerator tropes** — terminal aesthetics, neon-on-dark, "ship fast" hustle energy. The FAQ explicitly distances the house from this: "Not in the intense, mandatory-events way."
- **Sterile luxury-beige templates** — thin-serif members-club minimalism with no life in it. The warmth must come from real people and real photographs, not from beige alone.

## Design Principles

1. **People are the proof.** Real photos, real events, real partner logos carry the credibility argument. The design's job is to frame them beautifully, never to compete with them.
2. **One community, three doors.** Attendees, applicants, and partners each need a clear next step (RSVP, reach out, partner with us) from any page — without the site fragmenting into audience-specific funnels.
3. **A place, not a platform.** Chelsea Commons is a physical place in Manhattan. Specificity (street address, event photos, named people) beats abstraction every time; write and design like a neighborhood institution, not a network product.
4. **Understated confidence.** Ambition is demonstrated by what the community does, not claimed with superlatives or hype mechanics. Editorial restraint, no shouting.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

*(Defaults assumed — user was away for this round; upgrade to a stricter target if needed.)*

- WCAG 2.1 AA contrast as the working standard (≥4.5:1 body text, ≥3:1 large text).
- Every animation (marquee, scroll reveals, aurora, velocity text) needs a `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative.
- Semantic HTML and full keyboard navigability, including the mobile menu and accordion FAQ.
- Note: global scrollbar hiding (`scrollbar-width: none`) is a known accessibility trade-off worth revisiting.
